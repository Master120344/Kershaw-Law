H-2A & H-2B Visa FAQs

Navigating temporary work visas can be challenging. We've compiled answers to common questions to help you understand the H-2A and H-2B programs.

These visas allow U.S. employers to hire foreign nationals for temporary jobs. The H-2A visa is for temporary or seasonal agricultural work. The H-2B visa is for temporary non-agricultural work (e.g., landscaping, construction, hospitality). Both programs require employers to demonstrate that there are not enough U.S. workers who are able, willing, qualified, and available for the temporary work.

U.S. employers or agricultural associations who anticipate a shortage of domestic workers for temporary agricultural jobs can petition for H-2A workers. Key requirements include:

  • Demonstrating the need for workers is temporary or seasonal.
  • Showing that insufficient U.S. workers are available.
  • Ensuring employment of H-2A workers will not adversely affect the wages and working conditions of U.S. workers.
  • Complying with Department of Labor (DOL) regulations regarding recruitment, wages, housing, and transportation.

The H-2B visa is for temporary non-agricultural jobs. Employers must prove a temporary need (a one-time occurrence, seasonal need, or peak-load need) and that U.S. workers are not available. A significant factor for H-2B is the statutory annual cap set by Congress, which is often supplemented but still highly competitive. Demand consistently exceeds the cap, often leading to a lottery. Timely and accurate filing is critical.

Starting early is crucial. General timelines are:

For H-2A visas: Employers should ideally begin the process at least 75 days (but no fewer than 60 days) before the workers' intended start date. More lead time is always better.

For H-2B visas: Due to the cap, employers should start preparations **6 to 9 months** before the employment start date to plan around cap subjection periods.

Key steps in both processes typically include filing with the Department of Labor (DOL), conducting U.S. worker recruitment, and then filing a petition with U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS). Our firm manages each step to ensure an efficient and compliant process.

Employer costs can vary, but generally include:

  • Government Filing Fees: Fees payable to USCIS for the I-129 petition and other related costs. Employers should always check the current USCIS fee schedule.
  • Recruitment Costs: Expenses for advertising job opportunities to U.S. workers as required.
  • Legal Fees: We provide transparent cost estimates upfront, typically offering a flat-fee structure for standard petitions.
  • Worker-Related Costs:
    • Both H-2A & H-2B: Inbound and outbound transportation for workers who complete a certain portion of the contract.
    • H-2A Specific: Providing housing at no cost to H-2A workers.
    • Employers generally cover the workers' visa application fees.

We provide a detailed breakdown of anticipated costs during your consultation.

The initial period of stay granted to an H-2 worker matches the employer's certified temporary need, usually up to one year. Extensions are possible in increments, allowing a worker to remain in H-2 status for a maximum continuous period of up to 3 years. After reaching the 3-year maximum, workers must depart the U.S. for an uninterrupted period before being eligible to return under H-2 status.
